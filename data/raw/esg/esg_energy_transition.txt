This inconsistency has made it difficult to determine which plans are actually in place versus which ideas are still at the early concept stage.

There have been several announcements about switching sites to renewable electricity, but only a portion of the facilities have confirmed power purchase agreements. Some plants reported that over half of their electricity now comes from renewables, yet the figures appear to be based on estimates rather than metered data. In at least two cases, the energy provider changed mid year and the accounting team did not update their spreadsheets, so the reported renewable share may not reflect reality. There was also confusion about whether certificates from one region could be counted against global emissions targets, and different teams used different rules when preparing their numbers.

Meanwhile, long haul transport partners provided emissions summaries based on industry average factors, which raised questions about accuracy because some vessels used for shipments were older and less efficient than the dataset assumed.

Another team said electrification was not feasible because of range limitations, but provided no supporting data. A third team mentioned discussions with suppliers about battery supply chains, although there was no written record of what those discussions included or whether timelines were agreed.

There was an internal proposal to set carbon reduction targets linked to employee performance reviews. The proposal has not been formally approved, and drafts of the target framework were revised several times with no final version recorded in the governance system. Meeting notes suggest that senior leadership intended to revisit the issue after reviewing financial implications, but no follow up meeting has been scheduled.

Another region attempted a more detailed evaluation, but the scenarios used were outdated and did not reflect newer climate policies. A few members of the risk committee expressed concern that transition risks could have material financial implications, yet no formal escalation procedure exists to ensure consistent reporting to the board.

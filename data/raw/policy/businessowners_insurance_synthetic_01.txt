Synthetic Businessowners Insurance Practice Document

This document describes a typical package policy arranged for a small or mid sized business that wants cover for its premises, contents, and general liability under one combined contract. The insurer agrees to provide the insurance described, while the named insured agrees to pay the premium when due, keep accurate records, and comply with the policy conditions. Throughout the wording the words you and your usually refer to the business named in the declarations as the insured, while we, us and our refer to the insurance company that issued the policy. Some terms appear in quotation marks because they have special meanings defined somewhere in the text, although the definitions are not always located in one convenient section, and you sometimes have to read several parts of the booklet to understand exactly what is or is not included.
The first part of the coverage generally deals with property. The policy states that when a limit of insurance is shown for buildings, business personal property, or both, the insurer will pay for accidental direct physical loss to that covered property at the premises described in the declarations, as long as the cause of loss is one of those listed as covered and no exclusion applies. Buildings normally include the main structure at the location, completed additions, fixtures such as permanently installed machinery, and certain landlord items like appliances or furnishings provided for tenants. Business personal property usually refers to movable property used in the business, including furniture, stock, equipment, and sometimes property of others that is in the insured’s care, custody or control. Tenant improvements and betterments that the insured has paid for and cannot legally remove may also be treated as covered personal property, even though they are physically attached to the building.

The policy also references items that are not covered at all, even if they are located at the premises. Typical examples include aircraft, private passenger cars and trucks that must be registered for use on public roads, money and securities, land, growing crops, and certain types of electronic data or valuable records except as provided in specific extensions. There may also be restrictions on outdoor property such as fences, trees, shrubs and signs, with small sub limits and only if loss is caused by specific perils like fire, explosion, or vandalism. The wording tends to mix these lists of covered and non covered items with references to separate endorsements, so it is easy for a business owner to misunderstand what is actually protected unless they read carefully.
In addition to the basic building and contents cover, the policy usually contains a group of extensions that automatically broaden coverage up to certain limits. These can include debris removal when a covered loss creates extra expense to clear away damaged property, fire department service charges when the insured has to pay a municipal fee for a call out, and temporary removal of property to protect it from further damage. There may be specific provisions for valuable papers and records which reimburse the cost to reconstruct information on important documents if the originals are destroyed by a covered cause of loss, and for accounts receivable which address amounts the business can no longer collect from customers because its billing records were lost. Other extensions might relate to newly acquired or constructed property, personal effects of employees, business property temporarily off premises, glass damage, and the cost to recharge fire extinguishing systems after a covered event.

The policy explains how limits of insurance and deductibles apply. Generally, the most the insurer will pay for any one occurrence is the applicable limit of insurance for that class of property shown in the declarations. Some forms allow for inflation protection, where building and contents limits automatically increase over time based on an index, but this is subject to rules about how the index factor is applied. A basic deductible amount will usually apply to most property losses, and special deductibles may apply to certain perils or coverages like theft or windstorm. The wording notes that the insurer will not pay anything until the amount of loss exceeds the deductible, after which it pays only the amount above that figure up to the policy limit. Certain small extensions, such as fire department service charges or arson rewards, may be payable without any deductible.
A separate section deals with the conditions that apply to property claims. The insured is told not to abandon damaged property to the insurer, and must take reasonable steps to protect property from further damage once a loss has occurred. Duties in the event of loss include promptly notifying the company or its agent, describing how and when the loss happened, separating damaged from undamaged property, allowing inspections, and submitting a sworn proof of loss within a stated period if requested. The wording emphasises that legal action cannot be brought against the insurer unless all policy terms have been complied with. There are rules about how losses are paid, whether replacement cost or actual cash value applies, how building losses are settled if repairs are not completed, and how loss is adjusted when more than one policy covers the same property.
The second main part of the contract concerns liability. There is usually a coverage called business liability under which the insurer agrees to pay those sums that the insured becomes legally obligated to pay as damages because of bodily injury, property damage, or personal and advertising injury to which the insurance applies. The company also has the right and duty to defend the insured against suits seeking such damages, even if the allegations are groundless, as long as the claim potentially falls within coverage. The duty to defend ends when the applicable limit of insurance has been used up by payment of judgments or settlements or by medical expenses payments. The policy makes it clear that no other obligations or services are covered unless they are specifically described in a supplementary payments section.

Liability coverage is subject to many exclusions, some of which are general and others that are quite technical. Common exclusions include expected or intended injury, meaning harm that a reasonable person would expect from the insured’s deliberate acts, and contractual liability for obligations assumed under a contract except for certain insured contracts such as leases or sidetrack agreements. There are exclusions for workers compensation and similar laws, employer’s liability for injury to employees arising out of employment, and many forms of professional services. Claims arising from ownership, maintenance or use of aircraft, autos, or watercraft are largely excluded and are typically insured under separate policies. Pollution is usually excluded except for very limited exceptions, and there are special provisions relating to access or disclosure of confidential or personal information and data related liability, reflecting concerns about privacy and cyber risks. Loss of or damage to the insured’s own product or work, or recall of such items, is also excluded in most cases.

The policy sets out who qualifies as an insured under the liability section. If the named insured is an individual, both the person and their spouse are insured for business operations. If it is a partnership or joint venture, the partnership and its partners are insured, and for a limited liability company the company itself, its members, and managers are covered for certain activities. For corporations or other organisations, the entity is an insured, along with its executive officers and directors, but only with respect to their duties, and employees are insured for acts within the scope of their employment, subject to exclusions. No person or organisation is an insured with respect to the conduct of any partnership, joint venture, or limited liability company that is not named in the declarations. This structure can create gaps if the business changes its legal form but fails to update the policy paperwork.
The liability section also includes limits of insurance that control how much the insurer will pay for different types of claims. Typically there is an each occurrence limit that applies to all bodily injury and property damage arising from one occurrence, a separate limit for damage to premises rented to the insured, and a medical expenses limit that applies to payments made for minor injuries regardless of fault. There may be aggregates for products completed operations and for all other liability, which cap the total the insurer will pay during the policy period. The wording explains that these limits apply regardless of the number of insureds, claims, or persons bringing suits, and that once the limit is exhausted no further payments will be made until the policy is renewed with fresh limits.

Medical payments coverage provides that the insurer will pay reasonable medical expenses for bodily injury caused by an accident on premises the insured owns or rents, or due to its operations, even if the insured is not legally liable, as long as the expenses are incurred within a certain period and the injured person cooperates with any required examinations. This coverage is intended to quickly resolve minor injuries and reduce the likelihood of lawsuits, but it is also subject to an exclusion list and a maximum per person limit shown in the declarations.
There is a set of supplementary payments provisions where the insurer promises to cover additional costs associated with defending claims, such as expenses it incurs, the cost of certain bonds, reasonable expenses incurred by the insured at the company’s request, and interest on judgments. These payments are usually made in addition to the liability limits. The policy also contains general liability conditions that address duties in the event of an occurrence, claim or suit, including the requirement to promptly notify the insurer, forward legal papers, cooperate in the defence, and not voluntarily make payments or assume obligations except for first aid, without the insurer’s consent.

Finally, there are common policy conditions that apply to both the property and liability sections. These often include cancellation provisions describing how the insurer or insured may cancel the policy, change conditions, or decline to renew. There are clauses on concealment, misrepresentation or fraud which state that the policy is void if any insured intentionally hides or misrepresents material facts. The insurer reserves the right to examine and audit the insured’s books and records as they relate to the policy at any time during the policy period and for several years afterward, in order to verify exposures and premiums. There are also provisions about inspections and surveys, explaining that any inspections are for underwriting purposes and do not guarantee safety or compliance with laws. Other clauses address other insurance, transfer of rights of recovery against others to the insurer after a loss, premium audit for estimated premium policies, transfer of rights and duties under the policy, and conformity to state law where policy terms are adjusted to meet statutory requirements.
